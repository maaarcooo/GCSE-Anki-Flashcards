What is profit? | The reward for the risk that entrepreneurs take in providing a product/service.
How do you calculate gross profit? | Gross profit = Sales revenue − Cost of sales
How do you calculate net profit? | Net profit = Gross profit − (Operating expenses + Interest)
What does gross profit take into account? | The expenses directly incurred in the cost of production.
What does net profit take into account? | All of the business expenses (both cost of sales and operating expenses).
What is a profit margin? | The amount by which sales revenue exceeds the costs.
How do you calculate gross profit margin? | Gross profit margin = (Gross profit ÷ Sales revenue) × 100
How do you calculate net profit margin? | Net profit margin = (Profit for the year ÷ Sales revenue) × 100
What does gross profit margin show? | The proportion of revenue left over after the business has paid for its costs of sales.
What does net profit margin show? | The proportion of revenue left over after the business has paid all of its costs.
Why might a business with high net profit margins still have low total profits? | If sales are low, even a high net profit margin won't generate significant total profits.
What is the Average Rate of Return (ARR)? | A measure of the profit from a proposed capital project, expressed as a percentage.
When is ARR used? | When a decision is required about which of two projects should be pursued to generate the most profit.
How do you calculate ARR? | ARR = [(average annual profit) ÷ (initial outlay)] × 100
What is one advantage of using ARR? | It considers all of the net cash flows generated by an investment over time and is easy to understand.
What is one disadvantage of using ARR? | It ignores the timing of cash flows and the opportunity cost of the investment.
What is sales volume? | The number of products sold (physical units).
How do you calculate sales revenue? | Sales revenue = price × quantity sold
How do you calculate market share? | Market share = (Sales revenue of a business ÷ Total sales revenue in the market) × 100
How do you calculate the percentage contribution of a product to overall sales revenue? | (Sales revenue of product X ÷ Total sales revenue of all products) × 100
How do you calculate percentage change? | Percentage change = [(New value − Old value) ÷ Old value] × 100
How do you calculate average price? | Average price = (Sum of all prices ÷ Number of price points)
What is quantitative data? | Statistical numeric data that can be used to support decision making.
What's the difference between primary and secondary data? | Primary data is collected first-hand for a specific purpose, while secondary data has been collected by someone else.
What are some sources of quantitative data? | Graphs and charts, financial data, marketing data, tables or infographics.
What does a pie chart help businesses understand? | The breakdown of data into different categories as parts of a whole.
What does a scatter graph show? | Relationships between two variables, showing correlation.
What is a positive correlation on a scatter graph? | When an increase in one variable leads to an increase in another, showing as an upward trend.
What is a negative correlation on a scatter graph? | When an increase in one variable leads to a decrease in another, showing as a downward trend.
What types of financial data can businesses use? | Sales revenue, profit, costs, tax, interest rates, exchange rates, asset valuations, and bank balances.
What types of marketing data can businesses collect? | Data from surveys, focus groups, observation, customer feedback, retail/online footfall, and publications.
What does demographic data include? | Information about the market's population structure such as age, gender, and income statistics.
What does market dimensions data include? | Size of the market, market shares of competitors, rate of market growth, and average market prices.
What is investment data? | Data related to prices of commodities, exchange rates, and stock market performance.
What are three limitations of using financial data in decision-making? | Different interpretations of data, data becomes out of date quickly, and qualitative factors are ignored.
What is "window dressing" in financial data? | Presenting data in a way that gives a positive impression of performance while hiding negative aspects.
Why is financial data often out of date? | By its nature, financial data relating to sales, costs, and profit is out of date as soon as it is generated.
Why are qualitative factors important even when using financial data? | Businesses measure performance against both financial and non-financial criteria, and some goals (like social responsibility) are difficult to measure statistically.
